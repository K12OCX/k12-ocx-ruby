Name Understand Addition
Learning resource type Professional Development Resource
Description Some first graders struggle to understand the concept of addition. Below are five evidence-based practices you can use when teaching students how to add. They do not need to be used in any particular order. Research has shown that these high-impact strategies benefit all students—and are especially effective for students with learning and attention issues.
Creator NCLD

Lesson: Develop Fact Fluency With Addition

Learning resource type
LessonPlan

Activity: 1

Develop Fact Fluency With Addition Provide frequent opportunities for students to practice fact retrieval.

Activity: 2

The Challenge: Calculation Fluency Fluency with mathematical procedures is a common area of struggle for students with learning and attention issues. Speed in computation is a significant predictor of a student’s performance in solving word problems: With faster computation comes better problem-solving performance. The Growth Opportunity: Fluency and Conceptual Understanding Opportunities to practice rapid recall of addition facts will further strengthen fluency. Families can play addition bingo at home to help students build fluency.

Activity: 3

Addition Bingo A fun way to practice fact fluency is to play addition bingo. Give each student a bingo board. Instruct students to choose a number from 2 to 12 and write it in a random box on the board. Repeat until all boxes have been filled (without repeating any numbers). Pair up students and give each pair two dice. To play, Player A rolls the dice and adds the numbers together to find the sum. Player B should verify that the sum is correct. If students have the sum on their bingo boards, they can cover the square with a coin or other object. Students take turns until one player completes a row, column, or diagonal line and calls out “addition bingo.” Differentiate: For students who struggle with recalling addition facts, use oversize dice so they can count the dots as needed. Provide instruction on using a number line or other manipulatives to gradually diminish the use of counting and to work toward building their fluency.



Lesson: Practice Counting Strategies

Learning resource type
LessonPlan

Activity: 1

Practice Counting Strategies Review a progression of counting strategies. Start with “counting all” (a strategy that students are likely familiar with). Students count the first addend in an addition sentence, count the second addend, and then count both addends together. (If you choose to use the term “addend,” define it as “a number being added to another.”) For instance, for 4 + 2, students would count 1, 2, 3, 4, and then 1, 2, and finally 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. Then progress to “counting on.” Students start with the first addend and then count on to include the second addend. Again, for 4 + 2, students would say 4, and then count on 5, 6. This makes counting more efficient, as it skips the step of counting each addend separately.

Activity: 2

Give each student 5 counters that are red on one side and yellow on the other (or similar manipulatives). Put the counters in a line with 3 showing the red side and 2 showing yellow. Review “counting all” by leading students in counting all the red counters. Tell students to touch each counter as they count: 1, 2, 3. Do the same with the yellow counters: 1, 2. Then, lead the class in “counting all” of the counters together: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Next, explain that the “counting on” strategy will save them time when they count. This time, use 6 counters to show 2 red counters + 4 yellow counters. Lead students in naming the first addend: 2. Instead of counting the yellow counters separately, “count on” to include them with the first set: 3, 4, 5, 6. Explain to students that they have added the two groups. Ask them how many counters they have in all. Reveal that students can be even faster at “counting on.” Teach them to start with the greater addend in the addition sentence. To add the same 2 red counters and 4 yellow counters as above (2 + 4), they should start with 4 and then count on 2 red counters: 5, 6. This helps students become even more efficient in their counting. As an extension or an exit ticket, have students flip the counters to show any combination of red and yellow counters that still total 6 in all (or any number you select). Have them model “counting on” for you.

Activity: 3

The Challenge: Understanding Counting Principles There are five principles to counting: Stable order One-to-one correspondence Cardinality Abstraction Order irrelevance Students who struggle with one or more of these principles can have difficulty learning addition. The Growth Opportunity: A Toolbox of Counting Strategies As students work with addition, they will have to fluidly combine counting principles, so it is helpful to practice them together. Developing a counting strategy, and then progressively using a more efficient strategy, helps students learn the counting principles needed for understanding addition.



Lesson: Estimate Sums

Learning resource type
LessonPlan

Activity: 1

The Challenge: Flexible Thinking Flexible thinking is difficult for many students. Estimation requires flexibility in thinking because students are not asked to provide the actual answer to a problem. Instead, they are asked to make predictions about possible answers. The uncertainty inherent to predictions requires students to consider many possibilities, which is a key aspect of flexible thinking. The Growth Opportunity: Building Estimation Skills Estimation is a key skill in mathematics. Using a number line can help students improve both estimation skills and flexibility in thinking. Representing numbers and the relationships between numbers on a number line is also an important intermediate step in mathematical understanding. Number lines help to bridge the gap between concrete understanding (such as counting and moving objects) and mental math. Being skilled at using a number line has been shown to be one of the most significant predictors of future success in math.

Activity: 2

Estimate Sums Teach students to use a number line to estimate the Sum.

Activity: 3

Number Line Estimation Using 5-Groups Give students a copy of the number line from 0 to 10. Have them use their pointer finger to show the shaded section from 0 to 5. Instruct students to point to the number 5 as the middle of the number line. Explain that 0 to 5 are shaded to show a “5-group.” For students who struggle, practice identifying single numbers as closer to 5 or 10 prior to moving on to estimating sums. Next, prompt students to estimate the sum of 6 + 3 using the number line as a visual guide. Ask students if the sum will be closer to 5 or 10. Have them explain their responses. Students may say that the 6 already pushed past 5, so several more jumps on the number line would get them close to 10. (The focus here should be purely on estimating, not adding.) Repeat this procedure with other problems. Challenge students to explain how the number line helps them to estimate the sum.
